Marking Scheme

  • Every assignment you write in ENGL 255 will be evaluated for content, organization, mechanics, grammar, and style.
  • Although all five marking scheme categories are considered for every assignment you write in ENGL 255, each category has a different weight:
    • Content: 40%
    • Organization: 15%
    • Style: 10%
    • Mechanics and Grammar (grouped together): 35%.

I. Content

- refers to the following elements:
- a workable, properly composed thesis
- an awareness of audience and purpose
- a clear understanding and complete analysis of the topic
- a workable topic sentence for each paragraph (stated or implied)
- a thoughtful presentation of main supporting ideas in each paragraph
- use of appropriate quotations (where relevant)
- originality of ideas and expression
- evidence of critical thinking
- appropriate evidence of reading and research (where relevant)

II. Organization

- refers to the following elements:
- a clear, well-placed thesis statement
- clear, well-placed topic sentences (if stated)
- a variety of effective transitions to make the writing “flow”
- an appropriate and logical structure within each paragraph and the essay as a whole
- maintenance of purpose of the writing
- an introduction, a developed body, and a conclusion as appropriate within paragraphs and the essay as a whole

III. Mechanics

- refers to the following elements:
- spelling: correct and consistent
- punctuation: correct, consistent, and with appropriate variety
- capitalization: correct and consistent
- documentation: correct and consistent
- format: correct and consistent

IV. Grammar

- refers to the following elements:
- sentence formation (clauses and phrases appropriately formed and connected—no sentence fragments or run-onsentences, either fused or comma splices)
- word order and form
- verb tense, form, voice (active or passive), and mood (indicative, imperative, subjunctive)
- subject-verb agreement
- pronoun case forms and pronoun agreement with antecedent
- appropriate adjective and adverb forms
- parallelism
- appropriate use of modifiers
- direct and indirect speech

V. Style

- refers to the following elements:
- evidence of stylistic control
- writing at the appropriate language level (informal, general, formal)
- writing at the appropriate academic level
- writing appropriate to content, subject, purpose, and audience
- demonstration of effective tone and appropriate vocabulary
- effective sentence variety
- evidence of creativity
- length and complexity of sentences
- maintenance of consistent style

Common indicators of stylistic problems include the following:

  • shift of focus
  • monotonous repetition of one or two syntactical patterns
  • change in level or tone
  • pretension (attempt at outward show of ability that appears to be false or inaccurate)
  • use of slang expressions and clichés
  • choppiness (short, unconnected sentences)
